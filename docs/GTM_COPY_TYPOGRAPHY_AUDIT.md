# Sproutflow GTM copy and typography review

Reviewed July 20, 2026. This is a heuristic buyer-confidence review of the website and landing-page code, not a substitute for customer interviews, session recordings, or conversion data.

## Executive assessment

Before this pass, Sproutflow looked capable but worked too hard to sound valuable. The strongest trust signals were real client outcomes, direct founder access, visible pricing, fixed quotes, and clear ownership. Those signals were diluted by repeated claims about “enterprise-level” thinking, NYC/SF agencies, revenue engines, psychology, and being cheaper than larger agencies.

The likely buyer reaction was: “This seems credible, but I have to work to understand the exact offer and decide what to do next.”

### Buyer-confidence score before changes

| Dimension | Score | Assessment |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Offer clarity | 5.8/10 | Websites and internal systems were both present, but the relationship between them took too long to understand. |
| Trust | 6.4/10 | Real results, testimonials, founder access, data-handling detail, and fixed pricing helped. Broad superiority claims weakened the proof. |
| Relevance | 6.6/10 | The small-business audience was clear, but some language described Sproutflow’s pedigree instead of the buyer’s problem. |
| Ease of decision | 4.9/10 | Four packages, many benefits, repeated CTAs, and a long inquiry form created unnecessary work. |
| Readability | 6.1/10 | Body type was generally readable, but large serif headlines, dense cards, long hero copy, small text, and repeated decorative treatments increased load. |
| Overall buyer confidence | **5.9/10** | Credible enough to keep browsing; not yet clear and calm enough to become the obvious choice. |

After the implemented pass, the projected heuristic score is **8.1/10** for a qualified small-business buyer. That projection should be validated with analytics and five to eight buyer conversations.

## Positioning now in use

**Core promise**

Websites that win the right clients—and systems that make growth easier.

**Plain-language explanation**

Sproutflow designs and builds custom websites, CRMs, admin tools, and automation for small businesses. One founder-led partner from the first strategy call through launch and support.

**Supporting proof**

- Attributed client outcomes appear immediately after the hero.
- Buyers see real project ranges before they make contact.
- The site states that a fixed quote comes before work begins.
- Founder-direct delivery and ownership are explicit.
- The data-handling page explains access, exports, backups, and offboarding in plain language.

This position is more defensible than “enterprise strategy at small-business prices.” It says what Sproutflow builds, for whom, and why the combination matters.

## Wording that attracts qualified buyers

### 1. Concrete outcomes

Effective examples:

- “50% more qualified inquiries”
- “Cut repeat admin work”
- “Guide visitors toward one clear conversion path”
- “Track leads, customers, and jobs in a system shaped around the way your business actually works”

Why it works: a buyer can picture the change in their business. Outcome language is strongest when a real result, timeframe, or named workflow supports it.

### 2. Risk-reducing process language

Effective examples:

- “A fixed quote before work starts”
- “No design or launch surprises”
- “You work directly with the person responsible for the strategy and the build”
- “Best guesses are fine. We will clarify the details together.”

Why it works: service buyers fear unclear scope, missed timelines, disappearing vendors, and technical lock-in. Removing those fears is often more persuasive than adding another benefit.

### 3. Honest fit language

Effective example:

- “If price is the only deciding factor, a DIY builder may be the better fit.”

Why it works: it qualifies without insulting budget-conscious buyers. It also demonstrates judgment instead of pressure.

### 4. Clear ownership

Effective examples:

- “Own what we build”
- “Your site and accounts still belong to you”
- “You can leave with your data”

Why it works: ownership is a specific, believable trust signal that many small-business buyers already worry about.

### 5. Calls to action that match the actual next step

The primary CTA is now “Tell us about your project” or “Start a project,” because the button opens an inquiry form. “Book a discovery call” was inaccurate at the click point and created a small but meaningful expectation gap.

## Wording that hurts confidence

### 1. Borrowed prestige

Examples removed or retired:

- “Enterprise-level thinking”
- “Strategic thinking from NYC and SF agencies”
- “What works for Fortune 500 companies” as a repeated sales argument

Why it hurts: buyers cannot verify these comparisons, and they invite the question, “Why are you explaining your price instead of showing your work?” Relevant professional experience remains in the founder story, where it functions as evidence rather than the main promise.

### 2. Large, unsupported superiority claims

Examples removed or reduced:

- “Revenue-generating assets, not digital brochures”
- “The clear choice in your market”
- “Anyone can sell you a website”
- “Very few local providers can build…”

Why it hurts: the claim is larger than the evidence on the page. Strong case studies make competitor put-downs unnecessary.

### 3. Vague aspirational language

Examples removed:

- “Let’s start something beautiful”
- “Level the playing field”
- “How we grow together”
- “Digital engine”
- “Measurable momentum”

Why it hurts: these phrases sound positive but do not help a buyer predict the deliverable, process, or result.

### 4. Internal or technical language too early

Examples to keep below the top of funnel:

- “Multi-tenant system”
- “Technical architecture”
- “Next.js”
- “Content models”
- “API integrations”

Why it hurts: technical detail is useful when a buyer evaluates delivery risk, but it competes with the decision at the top of the page. The homepage now leads with recognized business tasks and keeps implementation detail lower in the journey.

### 5. Negative framing

Examples changed:

- “We don’t disappear after launch” became “Support that continues after launch.”
- “If you’re only looking for the cheapest…” became a neutral DIY-fit recommendation.

Why it hurts: negative wording plants the failure scenario in the buyer’s mind. Positive, specific commitments communicate the same benefit with less anxiety.

## Typography research

Leading B2C brands do not converge on one magic font. They converge on a system:

1. A distinctive display voice for recognition.
2. A quieter text face optimized for repeated reading.
3. Few typefaces, useful weights, and consistent hierarchy.
4. Enough flexibility to work from small UI labels to large campaigns.

Examples:

- Apple uses SF Pro as its system sans and advises against light weights and too many typefaces. Its guidance ties typography to legibility, hierarchy, and brand expression: [Apple typography guidance](https://developer.apple.com/design/human-interface-guidelines/typography).
- Airbnb introduced the custom sans-serif Airbnb Cereal across both product and brand to combine character, function, and scale: [Airbnb Design, “Working Type”](https://medium.com/airbnb-design/working-type-81294544608b).
- Spotify moved to the variable Spotify Mix to create a recognizable voice that can change across product and campaigns: [Introducing Spotify Mix](https://newsroom.spotify.com/2024-05-22/introducing-spotify-mix-our-new-and-exclusive-font/).
- Google separated display and text needs after finding its geometric display face was not legible enough at small sizes. Google Sans Text uses taller, narrower forms and more spacing for readability: [Making Google Sans Flex](https://design.google/library/google-sans-flex-font).
- YouTube documented that an overly expressive brand font began to overpower content, then refined tracking, leading, and character shapes. This is especially relevant to Sproutflow’s concern about overwhelm: [The making of YouTube Sans](https://design.google/library/youtube-sans-font).

The practical lesson is not “copy Airbnb’s font.” Those faces are proprietary and specific to their brands. The useful pattern is a restrained two-role system: recognizable display type plus highly readable text type.

## Font system implemented

### Display: Bricolage Grotesque

Used for primary headings and major section titles. It is a variable grotesque with enough irregular character to be recognizable, but it remains a sans-serif and reads cleanly at landing-page sizes. It replaces DM Serif Display, which pushed the site toward a familiar boutique-agency look and only loaded one real weight while components requested several synthetic weights.

### Body and UI: Source Sans 3

Used for paragraphs, navigation, controls, labels, pricing, and dense card content. Source Sans 3 was designed to work well in user interfaces: [Source Sans 3 on Google Fonts](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Source%2BSans%2B3).

### Why this pair fits Sproutflow

- The headline face feels crafted and contemporary without becoming ornamental.
- The body face has open forms and strong readability at 16px.
- Two sans-serif families reduce visual switching while preserving brand hierarchy.
- Both are open fonts and self-host through Next.js, avoiding third-party font requests at runtime.
- Variable font files provide real weights instead of synthesized bold text.

## Readability rules implemented

- Base body text: 16px with 1.65 line height.
- Reading measure utility: 68 characters for longer explanatory copy.
- Sentence-case headings throughout primary buyer journeys.
- Heading line height of about 1.1 and tighter display tracking; body copy keeps normal tracking.
- No light font weights for important text.
- Muted body color darkened to a measured 5.31:1 contrast ratio on white.
- Gold badge treatment changed because the prior white-on-gold combination measured only 2.61:1.
- Reduced-motion preferences now disable decorative transitions and animation.
- Visible keyboard focus is defined globally.

W3C guidance requires at least 4.5:1 contrast for normal text and supports line spacing between 1.5 and 2 for easier tracking: [WCAG 2.2 contrast](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#contrast-minimum), [W3C line-spacing technique](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Techniques/css/C21). W3C’s enhanced visual-presentation guidance also recommends text blocks no wider than 80 characters: [Understanding visual presentation](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/visual-presentation).

## Conversion and information-load changes

### Homepage

- Replaced prestige claims with a one-sentence offer.
- Added founder-led and New Orleans context as factual badges.
- Kept measured client results directly below the hero.
- Rewrote value propositions around comprehension, action, and measurement.
- Renamed the four process stages so each label states the actual work.
- Shortened business-system descriptions and removed competitor claims.
- Reduced package outcomes from two simultaneous claims to one per card.
- Converted the misleading “add-ons accordion” into actual collapsed, native disclosure panels.
- Removed a duplicated full-width CTA between the founder section and contact section.
- Replaced the oversized contact area with one project CTA and two direct contact options.

### Inquiry page

- Reduced ten visually separate question sections to two groups.
- Replaced long radio lists with compact, accessible select controls.
- Made it explicit that only name and email are required.
- Removed the optional phone request from the visible form.
- Explained what happens after submission and linked to the data-handling page.
- Changed the submit CTA from “Submit inquiry” to “Send project details.”

### Supporting pages

- Portfolio copy now describes live work and what every project includes.
- Case-study copy leads with verifiable outcomes.
- FAQ copy now explains when a template is genuinely the better choice.
- Process copy leads with fixed scope, approvals, and ownership rather than “enterprise methodology.”
- Calls to action consistently describe the inquiry action they open.

## Recommended validation after launch

1. Track hero CTA click-through, inquiry starts, inquiry completions, and qualified calls booked.
2. Compare inquiry completion rate and lead quality for 30 days before changing fields again.
3. Interview five recent buyers and three lost prospects. Ask them to describe Sproutflow after viewing only the hero, results bar, and first value section.
4. Test one hero variant focused on websites and one focused on the combined website-plus-systems offer. Keep the rest of the page unchanged.
5. Add timeframes and source notes to every percentage metric where client approval allows it.
6. Use a real client-work detail or founder-at-work image in the hero when a strong original asset is available; the generic seedling image supports the name but does less to prove the work.

## Brand voice guardrails

Sproutflow should sound like a capable owner-operator explaining a project across the table.

- **Direct, not blunt:** use plain verbs and name the next step.
- **Specific, not inflated:** prefer a workflow, timeframe, or deliverable to a superlative.
- **Reassuring, not defensive:** state ownership, scope, and support without invoking horror stories.
- **Expert, not technical by default:** introduce implementation detail only when it helps the buyer evaluate risk.
- **Warm, not whimsical:** one memorable phrase is enough; the rest should make the decision easier.

Preferred terms: project, website, business system, customer, lead, fixed quote, launch, ownership, support.

Use sparingly: strategy, conversion, platform, custom.

Avoid as positioning: enterprise-grade, digital engine, world-class, game-changing, beautiful journey, revolutionize, seamless ecosystem.
